# The Price of Courage, The Cost of Cowardice, and The Problem That is Maria. An autobiographical tale.

**Spoiler:** She ain't no problem.

---

I was born in a place that Tim Mcgraw would call a 'map dot, a stop sign on a black top'. 

I was born at home by midwifes, to a delightfully hippy Mother, and til the time I was 7 years old I was home schooled.

For me being home schooled was pretty cool, there wasn't a lot of structure in the education and much of it was at my own direction, but I had unfettered access to a full set of World Book encyclopedias and a 20 inch crt monitor where I could watch all the John Wayne movies I could get my hands on.

It wasn't a bad way to be introduced to the world. 

I had become accustomed to change in my first 7 years, because my family moved quite a lot in those days and by the time I was 7 I had called 5 houses across 3 states, home. I wasn't yet cognizant enough to know that this is the sort of thing that takes a toll on people, though I learned it by seeing, hearing, and juking my way through swinging arms that occasionally flew in my direction.

Eventually the days of my home schooling had ended and I was fortunate to have a mild transition when I was enrolled in a Christian School in the small Indiana town where we lived.

I remember very well my first day of 2nd grade when I met Mrs. Winslow, a tall and kind woman that certainly gave me a sense of ease. 

The Christian School was a lot like most schools, with reading, writing, and arithmetic. Unlike most schools, though, memorizing scripture was also a significant part of the curriculum.

The swagger I had learned from John Wayne mostly served me well among the adults at the school, because a 7 year old that lives by a code taught to him by The Duke can be quite charming; but among my classmates and peers I learned that encyclopedic knowledge coupled with the manners I had learned from my patron saint was as like to get me punched square in the nose as it was anything else.

I carried on in my new pedagogical milieu until about mid way through my 4th grade year. Things churned in my world, but I hardly noticed, because churn was all all I had known. My parents struggled to tolerate each other's presence and ultimately as Jerry Jeff Walker once said 'social pressure and loss of job fear...had them married and divorced...they couldn't cut it'.

As I started to notice the churn I began to become aware of circumstances and it was around this time that I committed my first crime. In the Christian School I attended there was a small bathroom with one stall that sat adjacent a giant wall painted an ugly tan color. One day as I sat all broken hearted I discovered that the can of Lysol which was kept behind the porcelain commode would leave a mark on that tan wall.

I decided then and there that this was my chance and so I spray 'painted' on that giant tan partition one word:

SHIT

That day and in the days that followed there was a rigorous man hunt to catch the culprit. I delighted in my mastery of the devious and narrowly escaped accountability. I guess that is why I didn't mind so much that we moved again, because I had learned that sometimes you had to get out of Dodge.

My family moved to a place in Indiana that the local residents often called 'B.F.E' ( for the sake of polity I will leave it to the reader to look that up ). Even though my Mother made a valiant effort to keep her children in that Christian School, ultimately practical realities as it relates to logistics and capital meant that I was to be enrolled in a public school. Due, in part, to the worldly knowledge I had gained from those encyclopedias and at my Mother's loving insistence I was enrolled in the 'gifted and talented' class at B.F.E elementary, the leaders of tomorrow I guess was the idea.

Public school wasn't too different from Christian elementary school, but scripture was not on the syllabus. As the new kid I was viewed by my classmates with curiousity and at times contempt, though I didn't understand why. On my first day at my new school I joined the other school children on the playground for recess where I mostly wondered around kicking the grass so to say.  I saw off in the distance a young girl who was an age appropriate peer sitting alone on a swing. Interesting, I thought, I wonder if she likes encyclopedias? I decided to go and find out.

I sidled up and set on the swing adjacent, doing my best Cary Grant impersonation ( I had learned a few things from him on that CRT monitor as well ) and I struck up a conversation. It wasn't long after I had successfully sparked the conversation that one of my grade school peers who went by the name J.J. wanted to join in the discourse. J.J. was a stocky kid that wore a sleeveless black shirt, baggy jean shorts, and shabby canvas sneakers. He had a countenance that could inspire fear in a 4th grader and cause a grown man to wonder who had hurt him. J.J. brought his own conversational style that included the prodigious use of the 'F' word. 

Mr. Grant took a breather and The Duke tagged in.

I didn't know much, but one thing I knew for certain was that you don't swear in front of ladies. I decided that I would make him aware of his faux pas so I stood out of my swing and looking at J.J I intended to say: 'It isn't polite to swear in front of ladies.'

I say I intended, because I guess J.J. had heard that one before and by the time I got out the first syllable he beat me to the punch as it were. J.J. pounced on me with a ferocity that I hadn't yet experienced, he took me to the ground and proceeded to pummel by body and face. If I was an egg then he was a whisk. 

SHIT

The teachers who monitored the playground rushed over and pulled J.J. off of me.

I learned that day the limitations as it relates to a moral compass guided by *The Man Who Shot Liberty Valance* and I was forced to pay The Price of Courage, on credit of course. 

---

Our house in B.F.E was a small white structure that evoked ideas of an old fashioned school house and it was situated on a five acre plot. The five acre plot was adjacent to what is called a 'rural route' and when I first arrived the street had no signs, you either knew where you were or you didn't.

Along that rural route the school bus, which I knew only as bus 19, would pick me up and begin its long winding path to the school which was about 10 miles away.

The school bus had its own hierarchy, with the young kids sitting up front and the high schoolers watching over it all in the very back.  Bus 19 was a social organism in its own right that included taunts, regular fights, and occasionally good conversation with a neighbor girl named Lindsey. I rode bus 19 daily for about 6 years, until I was 16 and could drive myself. As I got older and graduated the levels of the 'gifted and talented' I watched the seniors age out as I progressed slowly toward the back of the bus and kindergartners on their first day filed on to fill the seats I had left behind. By the time I made it to 8th grade I was sitting smack in the middle of the bus as was befitting my station as a middle schooler.

Save for the occasional conversations with Lindsey, I mostly kept to myself in quietude. I was still trying to workout the source of the contemptousness that my peers seemed eager to dole out and if ever I had forgotten that first day of 4th grade, my peers were always happy to regale me of the tale that was *The Ass Kicking of Thomas*. Along bus 19's route there was a sophomore aged kid named Derek and a 7th grade kid named Richard. I had seen them progress on their journey to the back of the bus since they were young, Richard being my junior and Derek being a couple of years my senior.

Richard was a kind hearted kid with a walk that could have easily been described as a waddle and he had light brownish hair that framed a chubby face.

Derek had blonde hair, a freckled face, and a toothy grin that under the right circumstances some might call endearing. Derek carried a chip on his shoulder that could of only been placed by someone who had mistreated him which was unfortunately common in B.F.E.

On one afternoon Derek was angrier than usual, which these days I'd wager had something to do with the fear of what waited for him once he stepped off the bus that afternoon. Derek decided that someone was going to feel his wrath and on that day the someone was Richard. Richard was literally reading a book, which was one of his favorite past times, when Derek took a seat next to him and began a relentless barrage of vile insults. Richard did his best to retaliate in kind, but he lacked the rhetorical skill and the wherewithall to cope with the unprovoked aggression from a boy driven mad from fear.

As Richard did his best to strike back with words Derek escalated with physical provocations daring Richard to, you know, make his day.

The school's administration had what they called a zero tolerance policy for fighting on the bus or otherwise, which meant that anyone involved in a fight, regardless of who instigated the confrontation would immediately be suspended from school and banned from the bus. I suppose the school's administrators thought it was a wise course of action to mitigate violence, but what it meant in practice was that kids like Richard were mostly left to their own devices.

I watched the whole interaction from beginning to end and I nearly mustered the courage to intervene, but I couldn't get banned from the bus and I was in no position to pay the price of courage again. I decided that Richard should fight his own battles.  As the bus progressed on its route and as we got closer to Richard's dropping off point Derek physically assaulted Richard by smacking his book out of his hands and aggressively pushing him. Richard did the best a 12 year old boy could to 'bow up' against the 16 year old Derek, but when he did Derek pushed him back down.

I watched. 

As we approached Richard's house I can still remember the look of fear, embarrassment, and betrayal on Richard's face when Derek spit the kind of loogie that only the natives of B.F.E know how to produce right on Richard's chubby cheeks. As Richard, stood up to disembark he did his best to hold back tears as he wiped the snot from his face and when he got off the bus he walked defeated to the door of his modest home.

I watched.

When I finally disembarked at my house, I walked through my front door carrying the shame that I now understand was The Cost of Cowardice.

---

As the years went on I guess I 'done alright', as Ray Wiley Hubbard might say. I finally got off bus 19 and started along a new path. Over the years I did my best impersonation of a grown man and even though I struggled to get the inflections right I think most people bought it. The path was winding and ultimately, thanks to that encyclopedic knowledge coupled with life experience I had learned the art of insolent competence which enabled me to climb the corporate ladder while keeping my ledger balanced.

By my 30's I had a 6 figure corporate job and a respectable title to boot, my impersonation of a grown man was getting pretty good. I decided that service to my community was something that I should look into, so I started to volunteer at a homeless shelter that focused on helping homeless men called [St. John's Center](https://www.stjohncenter.org/). St. John's Center is a homeless day shelter, where any man can take a hot shower, get a hot cup of coffee, and have a place to get mail. I began my volunteering career at the front desk where I would ensure each of the men signed in and handed out dry socks if we had any available. 

It was at St. John's Center where I met Maria.  

Maria is a petite woman with short grey hair and a silver cross that she wears around her neck. Maria was the executive director at the St. John's Center for homeless men where for a modest salary she spent every day in the beautiful stone church on Muhammad Ali Blvd in Louisville Kentucky that is the global headquarters for St. John's mission. To say that Maria is a kind woman is to expose the limitations of language, each day she would walk the floor showing genuine interest, care, and empathy to the haggard men that frequented the shelter.

Maria was a force and she had mastered the fine art that is the great power of a soft touch. It is as hard not to love Maria as it is to deny her a request so when she asked me to join the board of directors, I thought 'yeah, that seems like something a grown man would do' and so I did. For years I worked closely with Maria to facilitate the strategic and tactical objectives of St. John's Center for homeless men. Through late nights in the center and at local coffee shops I did my best to work with Maria as we put together a strategic plan to help kindness grow. 

Ultimately, through the weight of the days and the dissonance of circumstances my carefully curated impersonation began to show its limitations. I left the board and I lost touch with Maria, because I couldn't bear the thought that she would see behind the facade, The Problem That Is Maria.

---

Early one morning in my 8th grade home room class, the news broke that J.J. had died from a self inflicted gun shot wound. I did not like to hear that news and looking back I can't help but wonder that if I had learned the great power of a soft touch sooner, perhaps I could have made a friend that day on the playground and perhaps it might of saved J.J.'s life.  I don't know how it turned out for Richard and Derek, though I pray they are safe. Thanks to what I learned from Maria, I know they are loved.

Also thanks to what I learned from Maria, I know that The Price of Courage is a pittance compared to The Cost of Cowardice.

And that ain't no problem.






















I have joked that you always know the home schooled kid, because he is always the smartest kid in the corner.
